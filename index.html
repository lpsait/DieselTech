Crude Oil & Diesel Fuel

Learning Objective

Describe the origin and composition of crude oil, explain the fractional distillation process, and identify the key properties that make diesel fuel suitable for compression ignition engines.

What is crude oil?

Crude oil (also called petroleum) is a naturally occurring liquid found in geological formations beneath the Earth's surface. It formed over millions of years from the remains of ancient marine organisms — primarily microscopic algae and plankton — that died and sank to the ocean floor. Over time, layers of sediment buried this organic material, and the combination of heat and pressure slowly converted it into the complex mixture of hydrocarbons we call crude oil.

Crude oil is not a single substance — it is a mixture of thousands of different hydrocarbon molecules, ranging from very short (light) chains to very long (heavy) ones. It also contains small amounts of sulphur, nitrogen, oxygen, and trace metals. The exact composition varies significantly depending on where in the world it is extracted.

What is a hydrocarbon?

A hydrocarbon is a molecule made exclusively of hydrogen (H) and carbon (C) atoms. Carbon atoms can form long chains, branched structures, or rings, which is why thousands of different hydrocarbons exist. The length and structure of the carbon chain determines the molecule's physical properties — its boiling point, viscosity, energy content, and how easily it ignites.

Light vs heavy crude

Crude oil is graded by density (API gravity) and sulphur content. Light sweet crude (low density, low sulphur) is the most valuable — it produces more petrol, diesel, and jet fuel per barrel. Heavy sour crude requires more intensive refining. The price difference between grades can be significant, affecting fuel costs globally.

How distillation works

Crude oil is heated in a furnace to ~400°C and fed into the tower as a vapour/liquid mix. Different hydrocarbons condense at different temperatures as the vapour rises up the tower. Shorter molecules boil at lower temperatures, rise higher, and condense near the top. Longer molecules condense lower down or remain liquid.

From raw distillate to road diesel

The diesel fraction drawn from the distillation column is called straight-run gas oil. It cannot be used directly in a modern vehicle — it requires further processing to meet fuel quality standards. These secondary refining processes include:

ProcessPurpose
Hydrodesulphurisation (HDS)Removes sulphur compounds using hydrogen under pressure. Modern Ultra-Low Sulphur Diesel (ULSD) contains <10 ppm sulphur — down from 2,000+ ppm in the 1990s. Essential for protecting catalytic converters and SCR systems.
HydrocrackingBreaks longer hydrocarbon chains from the heavy oil fraction into the diesel boiling range, increasing diesel yield from each barrel of crude.
Cetane improvementCetane improver additives (e.g. 2-EHN) may be blended in to raise the cetane number to specification.
Cold flow additivesDiesel can wax (solidify) at low temperatures. Cold flow improvers and CFPP (Cold Filter Plugging Point) additives lower the temperature at which waxing occurs — critical for winter diesel grades.
Lubricity additivesULSD has lower natural lubricity than high-sulphur diesel. Lubricity additives are blended in to protect fuel pump and injector components from wear.
What ULSD means for technicians

Ultra-Low Sulphur Diesel is mandatory in the UK and EU. Running older, high-sulphur diesel — or contaminated fuel — in a modern engine will rapidly destroy the DPF, SCR catalyst, and injectors. Fuel contamination is a real-world diagnostic consideration.

Biodiesel (FAME)

Fatty Acid Methyl Ester (FAME) is produced from vegetable oils or animal fats. UK pump diesel is currently B7 (up to 7% FAME). Higher blends (B20, B30) are used in some fleets. FAME has higher lubricity but lower energy content and can degrade rubber seals in older fuel systems.

Key diesel fuel properties — click each to expand

Cetane Number
Measures ignition readiness. Higher = shorter ignition delay = smoother combustion. Standard road diesel: 51–54. Premium diesel: 55–60+. Low cetane causes hard starts, white smoke, and knock on cold mornings.
Density
Typically 820–845 kg/m³ at 15°C. Denser fuel contains more energy per litre. ECUs on modern engines use a fuel density sensor or temperature compensation to maintain accurate fuel metering.
Viscosity
Resistance to flow. Diesel must be viscous enough to lubricate the high-pressure pump but thin enough to atomise through the injector nozzle. Spec: 2.0–4.5 mm²/s at 40°C. Too thick = poor atomisation. Too thin = pump wear.
Flash Point
The minimum temperature at which diesel vapour can ignite with a flame. Diesel: >55°C (much safer than petrol at –43°C). This is why diesel does not readily ignite from a spark — it needs compression heat, not a naked flame.
CFPP
Cold Filter Plugging Point — the lowest temperature at which diesel can pass through a filter. Summer diesel CFPP: approx 0°C. Winter diesel: –15 to –22°C. Below CFPP, wax crystals block the fuel filter and starve the engine.
Energy Density
Diesel contains approximately 35.7 MJ/litre — around 15% more energy per litre than petrol. Combined with the higher thermal efficiency of diesel engines, this gives diesel vehicles significantly better fuel economy.
Sulphur Content
Modern ULSD: <10 ppm (parts per million) sulphur. Sulphur burns to form sulphur dioxide, which poisons catalytic converters and forms acid in engine oil. Low sulphur is essential for aftertreatment systems to function correctly.
Lubricity
The fuel's ability to reduce friction in precision fuel system components. Diesel lubricates the high-pressure pump and injectors as it flows through them. ULSD has inherently lower lubricity, so lubricity additives are mandatory in the blend.
Carbon chain length
Diesel hydrocarbons are predominantly C10–C22 chains (10 to 22 carbon atoms). This range gives the right balance of boiling point, energy content, and combustion characteristics for a compression ignition engine.
Misfuelling — diesel in a petrol engine and vice versa

Adding petrol to a diesel tank is a serious mistake. Petrol reduces the lubricity of the fuel, causing rapid wear of the high-pressure pump within minutes of running. The pump generates metal debris that contaminates the entire fuel system — injectors, common rail, and filter. If misfuelling is caught before starting, drain the tank. If the engine has run, full fuel system replacement may be required. The narrower filler neck on diesel pumps (22mm vs petrol's 25mm) is designed to prevent this, though diesel nozzles can still be inserted into petrol cars.

Combustion Principles

Learning Objective

Explain the difference between diesel and petrol combustion, and describe the conditions needed for diesel ignition.

What is combustion?

Combustion is a rapid chemical reaction between fuel and oxygen that releases energy as heat and light. In an engine, we capture that heat energy and convert it into mechanical motion.

The basic equation: Fuel + Oxygen → Carbon Dioxide + Water + Energy

For combustion to occur, you need three things — often called the "fire triangle": fuel, oxygen, and an ignition source (heat). Remove any one of these and combustion stops.

Petrol engines

Use a spark plug to ignite a pre-mixed air/fuel charge. The mixture is relatively lean and relies on an external electrical spark to begin combustion. Compression ratios are typically 8:1 to 12:1.

Diesel engines

Have no spark plug. Instead, air alone is compressed to such a high ratio (16:1 to 23:1) that it becomes hot enough to spontaneously ignite diesel fuel when it is injected directly into the cylinder.

Key Principle — Compression Ignition

Diesel engines work on the principle of compression ignition. The air temperature at the end of the compression stroke can reach 700–900°C. Diesel fuel's auto-ignition temperature is around 250°C — so it ignites immediately on contact with this superheated compressed air.

245 °C
Petrol territory — no ignition without a spark

Cetane number

Just as petrol has an octane rating (resistance to knock), diesel has a cetane number. Cetane measures how readily diesel fuel ignites under compression. A higher cetane number means quicker, cleaner ignition and smoother combustion.

Standard road diesel: cetane 51–54. Premium diesel: cetane 55–60+. Lower cetane can cause hard starting, white smoke on startup, and rough running.

Why is diesel more efficient?

The higher compression ratio means the fuel/air mixture is expanded further before the exhaust stroke. More expansion = more work extracted from each combustion event. This is why diesel engines produce more torque and better fuel economy than comparable petrol engines.

The Four-Stroke Cycle

Learning Objective

Describe each stroke of the diesel four-stroke cycle, identifying valve positions, piston movement, and what happens to the gas inside the cylinder at each stage.

Overview

A four-stroke diesel engine completes one full power cycle every two revolutions of the crankshaft. Each revolution involves two strokes (up and down movements of the piston). The four strokes are: Induction → Compression → Power → Exhaust.

Click each stroke card below to reveal the detail for that stroke.

1

Induction

Piston moves down. Inlet valve open, exhaust valve closed. Fresh air is drawn into the cylinder. No fuel yet. The cylinder fills with clean air ready for compression.
2

Compression

Piston moves up. Both valves closed. Air is compressed to 1/20th of its original volume. Temperature rises to 700–900°C. No fuel injected until near TDC (Top Dead Centre).
3

Power

Fuel is injected near TDC and auto-ignites. Rapid combustion expands gas, forcing the piston down. Both valves remain closed. This is the only stroke that produces useful work — it drives the crankshaft.
4

Exhaust

Piston moves up. Exhaust valve open, inlet valve closed. Burned gases are pushed out of the cylinder. At TDC, the exhaust valve closes, inlet valve opens, and the cycle repeats.

Animated piston diagram

Valve timing and overlap

Valves do not open and close exactly at TDC or BDC. There is a period called valve overlap where both inlet and exhaust valves are briefly open together, near TDC between the exhaust and induction strokes. This helps scavenge exhaust gases and improves cylinder filling.

TDC and BDC

TDC = Top Dead Centre — the highest point the piston reaches. BDC = Bottom Dead Centre — the lowest point. The distance the piston travels between these points is called the stroke, and defines the engine's displacement.

Multi-cylinder firing order

Most diesel engines have 4 or 6 cylinders. The cylinders fire in a specific sequence (the firing order) to keep the engine balanced and minimise vibration. A common 4-cylinder firing order is 1-3-4-2. This means cylinder 1 fires first, then 3, then 4, then 2, each separated by 180° of crankshaft rotation.

In a 4-stroke, 4-cylinder engine there is a power stroke every 180° — providing smooth, continuous power output.

Fuel Systems

Learning Objective

Trace the path of diesel fuel from the tank to the combustion chamber. Identify each component in the fuel system and describe its function.

The fuel path

Diesel fuel follows a precise path from the fuel tank to the injectors. Each stage conditions and pressurises the fuel before it enters the combustion chamber. Click each component below to learn its function.

Fuel Tank → Lift Pump → Fuel Filter → High Pressure Pump → Common Rail → Injectors

Click a component to explore it:

Common rail direct injection (CRDI)

Modern diesel engines use Common Rail Direct Injection. A high-pressure pump pressurises fuel to between 1,600 and 2,500 bar (for reference, a car tyre is around 2–3 bar) and stores it in a steel accumulator rail. All injectors draw from this common rail, allowing injection timing and quantity to be controlled electronically with extreme precision.

Older diesel systems (pump-line-nozzle) had mechanical injection timing linked directly to engine speed. CRDI allows multiple injection events per combustion cycle: a pilot injection, main injection, and post injection, which improves efficiency and reduces emissions and combustion noise.

Pilot Injection

A tiny amount of fuel injected before the main charge. This pre-heats the combustion chamber slightly, reducing the ignition delay and making combustion more gradual and quiet — reducing the characteristic diesel "clatter".

Post Injection

A small injection after the main event. Used to raise exhaust temperature for DPF (diesel particulate filter) regeneration, or to add a small amount of energy for emissions control. Does not contribute to power.

Injector types

TypeOperating pressureControl method
Mechanical (jerk pump)200–350 barMechanical camshaft
Solenoid CRDI1,600–2,000 barECU-controlled solenoid
Piezo CRDI2,000–2,500 barECU-controlled piezo crystal

Piezo injectors respond up to 5× faster than solenoid types, allowing more precise multiple-injection events and finer fuel atomisation.

Turbocharging & Air Management

Learning Objective

Explain how a turbocharger works, describe the role of intercooling, and identify how boost pressure affects engine performance.

Why turbocharge a diesel?

Combustion requires oxygen. More oxygen in the cylinder means more fuel can be burned, producing more power. A turbocharger forces more air into the cylinder than atmospheric pressure alone would deliver — this is called forced induction.

Virtually every modern diesel engine is turbocharged. The benefits are substantial: 30–50% power and torque increase over a naturally aspirated equivalent, with improved thermal efficiency.

How a turbocharger works

A turbocharger consists of two linked turbine wheels on a single shaft, housed in two separate casings: the turbine housing and the compressor housing.

Turbine side: Hot exhaust gases leaving the engine hit the turbine wheel, causing it to spin at speeds up to 250,000 rpm. The energy in the exhaust gases would otherwise be wasted — the turbo harvests it.

Compressor side: The spinning shaft drives the compressor wheel, which draws in fresh air and compresses it before sending it to the engine's inlet manifold. This compressed air is denser and contains more oxygen per unit volume.

0.0 bar boost
Naturally aspirated — atmospheric pressure only

Intercooling (charge air cooling)

Compressing air heats it. Hot air is less dense, so it contains fewer oxygen molecules per unit volume — partially offsetting the benefit of turbocharging. An intercooler (also called a charge air cooler) sits between the turbocharger compressor outlet and the engine inlet manifold.

Intercoolers work like a radiator: the hot compressed air passes through a matrix of tubes surrounded by ambient air (or coolant). Dropping the charge air temperature by 40–60°C increases its density significantly, improving combustion efficiency further and reducing the risk of pre-detonation.

The complete air path

Air intake → Air filter → Turbocharger compressor → Intercooler → Inlet manifold → Cylinder. On the exhaust side: Cylinder → Exhaust manifold → Turbocharger turbine → Catalytic converter → DPF → Exhaust pipe.

Variable geometry turbines (VGT)

A traditional turbocharger has a fixed turbine housing — it is efficient at a narrow range of engine speeds. At low rpm, exhaust flow is insufficient to spin the turbine fast enough (turbo lag). At high rpm, boost can become excessive.

VGT turbos (also called variable nozzle turbines, VNT) have adjustable vanes inside the turbine housing. At low rpm, the vanes partially close, accelerating exhaust gas onto the turbine wheel. At high rpm, the vanes open to allow greater flow. This provides strong boost across a much wider rev range and virtually eliminates turbo lag.

Wastegate

On fixed-geometry turbos, a wastegate valve diverts excess exhaust gases around the turbine at high boost pressures, preventing over-boost which could damage the engine or turbo.

EGR (Exhaust Gas Recirculation)

A portion of cooled exhaust gas is recirculated back into the intake. This dilutes the oxygen in the charge, lowering peak combustion temperature and reducing NOx (nitrogen oxide) emissions — a key factor in meeting Euro emission standards.

Engine Management Systems

Learning Objective

Identify the main sensors in a diesel engine management system, explain how the ECU uses sensor data to control fuel injection, and describe what happens when a sensor fails.

The ECU — electronic control unit

The ECU is the brain of the engine management system. It receives inputs from a network of sensors, processes that data against thousands of stored calibration maps, and outputs control signals to actuators (injectors, EGR valve, VGT actuator, etc.).

Modern diesel ECUs process sensor data at speeds exceeding 1,000 calculations per second, continuously adjusting injection timing, duration, and pressure to optimise power, efficiency and emissions for the exact operating conditions.

Key sensors — click each to expand

MAF Sensor
Mass Air Flow sensor — measures the mass of air entering the engine. The ECU uses this to calculate the correct fuel quantity for the desired air/fuel ratio.
MAP Sensor
Manifold Absolute Pressure sensor — measures inlet manifold pressure (boost pressure). Tells the ECU how much air is being delivered so it can adjust fuelling and VGT position.
CKP Sensor
Crankshaft Position sensor — reads a reluctor wheel on the crankshaft. Provides engine speed (RPM) and piston position data. Critical for injection timing. Engine will not run without this sensor.
CMP Sensor
Camshaft Position sensor — identifies which cylinder is on its compression stroke, allowing the ECU to synchronise sequential injection events.
ECT Sensor
Engine Coolant Temperature sensor — reports engine temperature. The ECU uses richer fuelling and different injection timing when cold. If this fails, the ECU often defaults to a fixed warm-engine strategy, causing poor cold starts.
Rail Pressure Sensor
Monitors actual fuel pressure in the common rail. If pressure drops or exceeds target, the ECU adjusts the high-pressure pump output. A faulty sensor can cause limp-home mode.
EGT Sensor
Exhaust Gas Temperature sensor — monitors exhaust temperature to protect the turbo and DPF. Critical during DPF regeneration cycles.
Lambda Sensor
Measures oxygen content in the exhaust. Diesel engines run lean (excess air), but lambda sensors help monitor combustion completeness and assist SCR (selective catalytic reduction) systems.
Throttle Position
Unlike petrol engines, diesels don't have a conventional throttle. The accelerator pedal position sensor (APP) directly informs the ECU of driver demand, which is translated into injection quantity and timing.
Limp-home mode

When the ECU detects a critical sensor failure or out-of-range reading, it may activate a "limp-home" or "reduced performance" mode. This limits engine power (sometimes to 1,500 rpm) to protect the engine from potential damage. A diagnostic tool is required to read the fault codes and identify the failed component.

Emissions after-treatment systems

Modern diesel engines must meet strict Euro 6 (or equivalent) emission standards. Several after-treatment devices work together:

DeviceWhat it does
DPF — Diesel Particulate FilterTraps soot particles from exhaust. Periodically "regenerates" by burning off accumulated soot at high temperature.
DOC — Diesel Oxidation CatalystConverts CO and unburned hydrocarbons into CO₂ and water using a platinum catalyst.
SCR — Selective Catalytic ReductionInjects AdBlue (urea solution) into exhaust to convert NOx into harmless nitrogen and water.
EGR — Exhaust Gas RecirculationRecirculates cooled exhaust gas into intake to reduce peak combustion temperature and lower NOx formation.

OBD — On-Board Diagnostics

All vehicles from 2001 (petrol) and 2004 (diesel) sold in Europe must be OBD-II compliant. The OBD system continuously monitors emission-related components and stores Diagnostic Trouble Codes (DTCs) when a fault is detected. A "check engine" or "engine management" warning lamp illuminates on the dashboard.

DTCs follow a standard format: P0XXX (powertrain), B0XXX (body), C0XXX (chassis), U0XXX (network). A diagnostic scan tool plugged into the OBD port (beneath the dashboard) reads these codes and provides freeze-frame data showing conditions at the time of the fault.

Faults & Diagnostics

Learning Objective

Recognise common diesel engine faults from their symptoms. Apply a structured diagnostic approach to identify the most likely cause of a fault before testing.

Diagnostic methodology

Good diagnostics is not guesswork — it is a systematic process. Always follow these steps before replacing any component:

1. Gather information

Talk to the driver. When does the fault occur? Any recent work? Warning lights? Unusual smells or sounds?

2. Verify the complaint

Replicate the fault yourself if possible. A fault you cannot reproduce is harder to diagnose reliably.

3. Retrieve fault codes

Connect a diagnostic scan tool to the OBD port. Read and record all stored DTCs and freeze-frame data.

4. Research the code

Understand what the code means. A P0191 (rail pressure sensor range) could mean a faulty sensor or a genuine low rail pressure — they must be differentiated.

5. Test before replacing

Use live data, a multimeter, or a pressure gauge to test the suspect component. Never replace a component on a code alone without verification.

6. Fix, verify, clear

Complete the repair, clear the fault codes, and verify the fix under the conditions that originally caused the fault.

🚗 Scenario 1 — 2019 Ford Transit 2.0 TDCi
Symptom: Hard starting when cold, white smoke for 2–3 minutes after start, then clears. No fault codes stored. Running fine once warm.
Click to reveal likely cause →
Likely cause: Failed glow plug(s). Glow plugs pre-heat the combustion chamber for cold starting. When they fail, there is insufficient heat to achieve auto-ignition quickly, leading to a long crank, incomplete combustion (white smoke from unburned fuel) and rough running until the engine warms via normal combustion. No fault code is common because glow plug monitors vary by vehicle. Test: glow plug resistance test with a multimeter — a working plug reads 0.5–2 ohms; an open circuit or very high reading = failed plug.
🚗 Scenario 2 — 2016 Volkswagen Passat 2.0 TDI
Symptom: Intermittent loss of power, engine enters limp mode at higher boost demands. DTC stored: P0299 — Turbocharger underboost. No smoke. Boost builds then drops.
Click to reveal likely cause →
Likely cause: VGT actuator fault or sticking VGT vanes. P0299 means the MAP sensor is reading lower boost than the ECU commanded. On a VGT-equipped engine, the most common causes are a faulty VGT actuator (electronic or pneumatic), or carbon-clogged variable vanes that are sticking and not responding to ECU commands. Secondary check: inspect for boost leaks (split intercooler hose, loose jubilee clip). Live data test: observe MAP sensor vs target boost — if target is high but actual is low, the turbo or actuator is the fault. Vane cleaning with specialist additive or physical cleaning is often the repair.
🚗 Scenario 3 — 2014 BMW 320d F30
Symptom: Heavy black smoke under acceleration, poor power, fuel consumption increased. DTC: P0401 — EGR flow insufficient. Air filter recently replaced.
Click to reveal likely cause →
Likely cause: EGR valve stuck open (or heavily carboned) causing excessive exhaust gas recirculation. Despite the code suggesting EGR flow is insufficient, a partially stuck-open EGR can flood the intake with exhaust gas, displacing fresh air and causing rich-running symptoms (black smoke, poor power). P0401 can be triggered by an EGR valve that is not reaching its commanded position — stuck open produces black smoke and power loss. Verify with live data: observe EGR valve position vs commanded position. A blocked or stuck valve requires cleaning or replacement. Also check intake manifold for carbon build-up which is common on direct injection diesels.
🚗 Scenario 4 — 2018 Mercedes Sprinter 2.2 CDI
Symptom: DPF warning light illuminated. Power reduced. Vehicle has been used predominantly for short journeys. No active fault codes beyond DPF soot load warning.
Click to reveal likely cause →
Likely cause: DPF blocked due to failed passive/active regeneration. The DPF captures soot particles. In normal driving, regeneration occurs passively (sustained motorway speeds generating exhaust temperatures above 600°C) or actively (ECU injects extra fuel to raise exhaust temperature). Short, slow urban journeys never allow the DPF to reach regeneration temperature, so soot accumulates. Resolution: a forced regeneration using a diagnostic tool (stationary regen), or a 30-minute sustained motorway drive. If severely blocked (soot load >75%), cleaning with specialist DPF cleaner fluid may be needed. Note: continued ignore may require DPF replacement — a £1,000+ part on many vehicles.
